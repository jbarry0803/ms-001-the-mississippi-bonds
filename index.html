The Situation at a Glance

In 1838 the State of Mississippi sold $5 million in bonds to capitalise a new state-owned bank. By 1842 the legislature had walked away from the debt — the first repudiation of a state debt in U.S. history. The bonds still exist; the state still refuses to pay.

The original revival theory was simple and elegant: buy up the bonds cheaply, then chase Mississippi's commercial assets in friendly foreign jurisdictions the way Argentine creditors chased Argentina. The 11th Amendment shut the U.S. courthouse door 200 years ago, but a foreign court might attach a state office in Frankfurt or a state-owned ship in Hamburg.

The first half of the theory still holds. Hundreds of bonds are sitting in a single dealer's vault in Portsmouth, New Hampshire, available at $700 each — down from $3,000 in 2013. Acquisition is realistic.

The second half is weaker than expected. A pass through Mississippi's published financial statements and overseas presence shows that almost nothing the state operates abroad is actually owned by the state. The Asset Audit tab walks through what was checked.

A debt that never quite died

The Mississippi default is, in the long history of sovereign credit, one of the cleanest examples of a state walking away from a debt it could plainly have paid. The state was not bankrupt. It collected enough property tax in 1845 to comfortably service the Union Bank bonds. It chose not to.

The mechanics were these. In 1831–33 the state issued roughly $2 million in 6% bonds to capitalise the Planters Bank. In 1838 it issued a further $5 million in 5% bonds to capitalise the Mississippi Union Bank. Both issues were largely placed in London and on the Continent — the coupons were even printed payable in London, an open invitation to British capital. By 1841 Mississippi had stopped paying. By 1842 the legislature declared, technically, that there was no obligation. And in 1875 the state constitution was amended to bar any future legislature from ever paying or recognising the bonds.

The British creditors organised. The Council of Foreign Bondholders — later the Corporation of Foreign Bondholders, founded in London in 1868 — pursued the matter for over a century. Letters went to successive Mississippi governors and were not answered. In the 1980s, the Council managed brief correspondence with the State Treasurer, who actually visited London. Nothing came of it. The Council closed its doors in the late 1980s, having recovered more from czarist Russian railway bonds than from Jackson, Mississippi.

"The British bondholders are getting a return on their investment, finally. The certificates, as collectibles, are worth something." — Clifford Thies, NPR, 2015

Most of the auctioned bonds ended up with George H. LaBarre Galleries in Portsmouth, New Hampshire, where they have sat for thirty-five years. They are the substrate of any modern revival.

The Inflation Floor · the boring number

$2,000 in 1838 dollars equals roughly $70,000 in 2026 dollars at long-run CPI (~35× over 188 years). This is what one bond would be worth if you stripped the coupon entirely and just preserved purchasing power.

The point of the contrast: most of the headline compounded number is unpaid interest, not inflation. The state didn't lose value to time. It lost it to refusing to pay.

The Recovery History · what was actually paid

Across 185 years: $0 from Mississippi. The Council of Foreign Bondholders in London pursued the matter for over a century and recovered more from czarist Russian railway bonds than from Jackson. Their inventory was liquidated at the Phillips London auction in 1990 — at collector prices, not face. The only realised "yield" the paper has ever had is the secondary collector market, exactly what Clifford Thies meant in the History pullquote.

The honest verdict

Combining the legal framework, the ownership audit, and the asset register — where this leaves a revival effort.

Obstacle 01
11th Amendment

Foreign citizens still cannot sue a U.S. state in federal court without that state's consent. The single rule that closed the courthouse door for nearly two centuries.

Obstacle 02
The 1875 Bar

The Mississippi state constitution still expressly forbids the state from paying or recognising the Union Bank or Planters Bank bonds. As a matter of Mississippi law, the obligation is void.

Obstacle 03 (audited)
Empty Foreign Asset Register

Across roughly $53B in state-controlled assets — pension fund, university capital, treasury, port, aircraft, vessels, trade offices — nothing is located abroad in attachable form. The Treasury is statutorily restricted to US securities. PERS is held in trust by US custodians. All physical capital sits in Mississippi. The trade "offices" are consultant contracts. The Argentine playbook needs targets; this is a desert.

Path remaining
Owned vessels in opportunistic transit

The four USM research vessels are state-owned. R/V Point Sur has historically transited Drake Passage to Antarctica. A vessel arrest in a friendly jurisdiction during such a transit remains theoretically possible, but it requires waiting on operational deployment patterns we don't control.

The original revival theory wasn't wrong — it was right for 1990. By 2026 the state has restructured how it operates abroad, and the targets the Council of Foreign Bondholders might have chased no longer exist as state property.

Realistic Next Moves

i

Acquire the bonds anyway

At sub-$300 per bond on volume, the historical, political and reputational asset has value independent of any litigation outcome. The bonds are themselves the campaign object.

ii

Reframe as political pressure, not asset attachment

Moody's rates Mississippi Aa2; Fitch rates AA. A well-organised holder of repudiated state debt — even debt the state insists is void — is an embarrassment a credit-rating-conscious state may eventually choose to settle. The Council of Foreign Bondholders ran this play on Mississippi for 100 years; modern PR cycles are faster.

iii

Monitor vessel deployment for foreign-port calls

R/V Point Sur AIS data is publicly available. A foreign port call would create a one-time window for a creditor with bonds in hand and counsel on standby in the relevant jurisdiction. Cheap to set up; uncertain to trigger.

iv

Pursue federal-level theories not previously tested

Mississippi receives ~$13B annually in federal funds. Whether a foreign creditor could ever interpose between the federal government and an in-default state recipient is a constitutional question that's never been litigated — because no one with standing has tried.
