The Situation at a Glance

In 1838 the State of Mississippi sold $5 million in bonds to capitalise a new state-owned bank. Within two years the bank was insolvent. By 1842 the legislature had walked away from the debt — the first repudiation of a state debt in U.S. history.

The bonds did not vanish. Hundreds are sitting in a single dealer's vault in Portsmouth, New Hampshire — the residue of a 1990 London auction by the liquidators of the Corporation of Foreign Bondholders. They can be bought for the price of a decent dinner.

This file sets out a two-step revival: acquire the paper at wholesale, then map the State of Mississippi's commercial assets abroad — the only category of property realistically reachable by a creditor.

A debt that never quite died

The Mississippi default is, in the long history of sovereign credit, one of the cleanest examples of a state walking away from a debt it could plainly have paid. The state was not bankrupt. It collected enough property tax in 1845 to comfortably service the Union Bank bonds. It chose not to.

The mechanics were these. In 1831–33 the state issued roughly $2 million in 6% bonds to capitalise the Planters Bank. In 1838 it issued a further $5 million in 5% bonds to capitalise the Mississippi Union Bank. Both issues were largely placed in London and on the Continent — the coupons were even printed payable in London, an open invitation to British capital. By 1841 Mississippi had stopped paying. By 1842 the legislature declared, technically, that there was no obligation. And in 1875 the state constitution was amended to bar any future legislature from ever paying or recognising the bonds.

The British creditors organised. The Council of Foreign Bondholders — later the Corporation of Foreign Bondholders, founded in London in 1868 — pursued the matter for over a century. Letters went to successive Mississippi governors and were not answered. In the 1980s, the Council managed brief correspondence with the State Treasurer, who actually visited London. Nothing came of it. The Council closed its doors in the late 1980s, having recovered more from czarist Russian railway bonds than from Jackson, Mississippi.

"The British bondholders are getting a return on their investment, finally. The certificates, as collectibles, are worth something." — Clifford Thies, NPR, 2015

Most of the auctioned bonds ended up with George H. LaBarre Galleries in Portsmouth, New Hampshire, where they have sat for thirty-five years. They are the substrate of any modern revival.

What hasn't changed

The obstacles are real and largely unchanged since 1841. They are not, however, insurmountable in 2026 — the modern sovereign-debt litigation playbook didn't exist when the Council of Foreign Bondholders was writing its unanswered letters.

Obstacle 01
11th Amendment

Foreign citizens still cannot sue a U.S. state in federal court without that state's consent. This is the single rule that closed the courthouse door to the British bondholders for nearly two centuries.

Obstacle 02
The 1875 Bar

The Mississippi state constitution still expressly forbids the state from paying or recognising the Union Bank or Planters Bank bonds. As a matter of Mississippi law, the obligation is void.

Path 01
Foreign-Court Attachment

The only viable path. Depends on (a) a foreign court treating the bond as recognisable debt, and (b) the targeted asset being commercial in character rather than diplomatic. Untested for U.S. sub-sovereigns — but the Argentine sovereign-debt precedents (NML Capital, 2010s) developed exactly this technique.

Path 02
What's New Since 1990

German courts have grown more receptive to bondholder claims generally. International AIS data makes vessel asset-tracking trivially cheap. Public corporate registries make commercial office leases identifiable in hours. The infrastructure for a revived collection effort exists in a way it simply didn't when the CFB folded.

The shape of the strategy — consolidate the bonds cheaply, then chase commercial assets in friendly foreign jurisdictions — is the same shape the Argentine creditors used. The legal innovation needed is the application of that playbook to a U.S. state.
